Proficiency is the difference between talent and practiced craft. It gives the game room for background, profession, and old scars to matter.

In Ka-Tet, proficiency should feel like lived history. A Calla farmer knows different things than a Lud scavenger. A former stage performer reads a crowd differently than a gunslinger does. The bonus exists so characters are not reduced to raw ability scores when their actual past would clearly matter.

## When it applies

Add proficiency when training, tools, background, archetype, or repeated real experience genuinely support the attempt. The book's character creation guidance leans this way too: backgrounds are supposed to carry a little real weight.

Good examples:

- A Tech-Knight reading warning labels on Old Ones machinery
- A Walker finding food or judging the weather
- A medic cleaning and stitching a wound during rest
- A Tongue of Discord controlling a room with performance or persuasion

## When it does not

Do not add proficiency just because the player wants the moment badly or because the scene is important. If the character has no clear training, the roll should stand on raw ability, luck, ka, or help from someone who does know what they are doing.

## Why it matters here

The world is harsh, and the party survives by being specific people with specific histories. Proficiency is one of the cleanest ways the rules reward that specificity.
