A thinny is never just scenery. It is an invitation and a threat.

Todash movement should feel useful, unstable, and costly. Players should come away feeling that the universe gave them passage, not permission.

The book describes thinnies as weak places where reality grows thin and starts to sound wrong before it visibly tears. They often hum, keen, or warble. Get too close and they can pull the unwary into Todash space, the black between-world limbo full of distance, bells, and hungry things.

## What thinnies do in play

- Block a route and force the long way around
- Create a temptation to cut across danger for speed
- Pull someone into a different world, dream, or echo
- Trigger fear, madness, or memory damage before anyone even crosses

The safest answer to a thinny is usually respect. Go around it, quiet it if you can, or close it with the right kind of magic, resonance, or plot device. Bravery alone is a poor plan.

## Going Todash

Todash travel can be accidental or deliberate. Doors, relics, dreams, psychic force, and major cosmological damage can all create a crossing. It should never feel routine. Even successful passage ought to cost time, certainty, or emotional stability.

Good Todash consequences include:

- Lost hours or altered time pressure
- A memory going missing or arriving blurred
- A new omen, scar, or unwanted attention
- A destination reached physically but not spiritually unchanged

If the party uses Todash travel to solve a problem, let it also create a second problem worth worrying about.
