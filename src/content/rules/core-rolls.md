Every risky action begins with a simple question: what are you trying to do, and what stat best matches that intent?

When the outcome matters, roll a `d20`, add the relevant ability modifier, and compare it to the difficulty. If your training, archetype, or equipment clearly matters, add proficiency too. Attacks work the same way, except you compare the roll to a target's Armor Class instead of a general difficulty.

## Degrees of outcome

- A clean success does what you hoped.
- A success with cost gets the job done but introduces pressure.
- A failure changes the scene instead of stopping it cold.

## What the GM is usually deciding

- Is this a normal check, an opposed roll, or an attack roll?
- Which ability fits the intent best?
- Does proficiency apply because of background, training, tools, or class?
- Does the scene grant advantage, disadvantage, or a consequence on failure?

The game works best when failed rolls still move the fiction. Missing the clue is usually less interesting than finding it too late, making noise, attracting attention, or paying for it in some other way.
