Rest should feel earned. In Ka-Tet, healing is about whether the party can create safety long enough for bodies and minds to remember how to settle.

The book leans toward slower recovery than high-fantasy dungeon games. Short rests let characters bind wounds, spend Hit Dice, reset certain class resources, and catch their breath if they have water, supplies, or a little help. Long rests restore more, but unsafe conditions can spoil the benefit.

## Short rests

An hour of relative calm is enough to patch bullet grazes, splint a twisted leg, clean a gun, or stop the worst bleeding. This is where grit comes back in practical, human ways. If the group has medicine, water, a fire, or someone with a healing-oriented talent, short rests should feel meaningfully better.

## Long rests

A full night's sleep is powerful only if the fiction supports it. In a friendly town, warded bunker, or properly guarded camp, long rests can restore health, class resources, and a little faith. In a haunted ruin, active war zone, or thunder-sick wasteland, sleep may not clear exhaustion or fully refresh powers at all.

Set watch rotations. Let camp security matter. A party that sleeps badly in a bad place should feel that choice the next day.

## Downtime healing

Wounds in this setting often want time, supplies, or ritual attention. Apothecary remedies, herbal brews, Devil Grass salves, medical talent, song-based healing, and careful maintenance all belong here. The world wants injuries to mean something, so recovery can be practical, slow, and occasionally weird.

Downtime is also when the ka-tet cleans guns, repairs harnesses, studies relics, brews medicine, and trains. That quiet labor is part of survival, not a break from it.
