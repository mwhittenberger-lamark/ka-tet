Horror in this game is about moral and psychic instability, not only body horror. Fear should push characters toward revelation, denial, or sacrifice.

Use these rules when the scene wants a cost that wounds judgment, not flesh. The book points especially toward thinnies, Todash phenomena, undead, Prim-born creatures, and impossible revelations as the kind of thing that should call for Wisdom saves or similar mental pressure.

## What fear should do

- Make a character hesitate, freeze, flee, or misread the room
- Leave behind tremors like nightmares, rituals, or small compulsions
- Push the party toward care, grounding, or ka-tet support

In the short term, failed fear saves often look like the **Frightened** condition, panic, stunned silence, or temporary madness. In the longer term, the consequence might be recurring dreams, obsessive behavior, or a visible psychological scar.

## When to call for it

Ask for fear or madness pressure when the danger attacks identity as much as survival. A gunfight in a street may not require it. Watching a dead thing walk, hearing a thinny sing your name, or realizing the room around you is only half in the same world probably should.

The gritty tone matters here. Mid-World is full of sights that can shake even a hardened gunslinger, and the cost should sometimes linger after the initiative order ends.

## Recovery

Recovery should come through bonds and quiet, not only through numbers. A safe camp, a Singer's calming refrain, a trusted conversation, or an undisturbed night's rest may reduce or clear the worst effects. Some wounds of the mind remain longer than hit point loss, which is part of what makes them useful in play.
