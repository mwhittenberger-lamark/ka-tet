The road is part of the genre. Long stretches of movement should reveal tone, shift relationships, and leave marks on the party.

Travel in Ka-Tet is not filler between fights. The book treats it as a pillar of play: who navigates, who keeps watch, who forages, how the group handles weather, and what price it pays for moving quickly all matter.

## What travel scenes should do

- Reveal the state of the world
- Pressure resources like food, water, ammunition, and sleep
- Create choices about speed, safety, secrecy, and route
- Give the ka-tet time to bond, argue, confess, or dream

## Common pressures

Mid-World is huge, old, and unreliable. Distances can feel wrong. Roads bend strangely. Weather can turn lethal. A thinny can force a major detour. Thunderclap and other sick lands may threaten fatigue, fear, or madness before a single enemy appears.

Foraging, camping, maintenance, and watch rotation all belong here. A good night's rest, a repaired revolver, or a clean campsite can matter as much as winning the last fight.

## Downtime matters too

Downtime is when gear gets patched, wounds get treated, songs get written, relics get studied, and ka-tet bonds get stronger or weaker. Let the road breathe. Those quiet stretches are part of what makes the violence feel costly.
