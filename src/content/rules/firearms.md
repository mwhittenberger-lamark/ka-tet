Firearms are rare enough to feel mythic and common enough to define the tone. They should command respect.

The book treats guns as a signature part of the setting rather than just another ranged option. A standard revolver hits hard, holds six shots, and can decide a scene before anyone closes to melee. Rifles dominate open ground. Shotguns own cramped rooms. Bows and thrown weapons remain useful because they are quieter and easier to resupply.

## What players should track

- **Capacity:** most sidearms and long guns do not fire forever.
- **Reload time:** a reload often costs an action unless a feature says otherwise.
- **Ammo scarcity:** bullets are treasure in a world that has forgotten how to make plenty.
- **Misfires:** old, dirty, or unstable guns can betray you.

By default, the book suggests a firearm misfires on a natural `1`. The shot fails, and the weapon usually needs an action to clear before it can fire again. Fine relic guns may be more reliable. Junk weapons may be worse.

## Why cover matters more than armor

Bullets make ordinary armor feel unreliable. A leather coat or scrap vest can help against claws, knives, or debris, but a wall, wagon, or steel door is often better protection than what you are wearing. That is why Ka-Tet firefights should look like dives behind stone, desperate reloads, and short windows of exposed violence.

Treat every bullet as both a tactical resource and a narrative signal. Once guns come out, conversations harden and bystanders start making choices.
