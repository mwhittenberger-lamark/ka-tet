Lud is what happens when technology outlives stewardship. It is useful, haunted, and never fully under control.

Once a booming city of the Old Ones, Lud now survives as a crumbling deathtrap on the River Send. Towers rot above streets full of bad wiring, old explosives, dead transit systems, and cultural habits that kept going long after they stopped making sense.

Two major survivor factions haunt the city's bones: the Pubes, a ragged younger population trying to live amid collapse, and the Grays, older war-broken loyalists who cling to a ruined order through violence and ritual. Their conflict makes Lud feel less like a dead ruin and more like a wound that still moves.

## What makes Lud memorable

- Ancient machinery is everywhere, and almost none of it is trustworthy.
- Drum tracks and speaker-noise turn whole districts into psychological pressure cookers.
- Collapses, traps, and hidden passages matter as much as enemy factions.
- Blaine the Mono hangs over the city as a symbol of old intelligence gone dangerously wrong.

In play, Lud should feel like Mad Max pressed through a dying myth. It is the kind of place where a single working terminal, train line, bridge, or vault door can reshape an entire session, but every useful discovery comes with the risk of waking something better left buried.
